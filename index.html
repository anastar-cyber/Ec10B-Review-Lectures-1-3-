LECTURE 2 · GDP IDENTITIES
Y ≡ C + I + G + (X − M)
Expenditure-based GDP identity. C = Consumption (68%), I = Investment (18%), G = Gov't Spending (16%), X−M = Net Exports (−3%). All spending on final goods.
Note: imports in C/I/G are subtracted via −M
Production ≡ Expenditure ≡ Income
Three equivalent ways to measure GDP. Equal by definition — every dollar produced is either spent or earned as income.
GDP = $29.3T in 2024
Value Added = Output − Inputs
Avoids double-counting. GDP = sum of value added at each stage = final sale price.
Cotton ($5) → Yarn ($4 VA) → Sweater ($31 VA) → GDP = $40
LECTURE 3 · MONEY & INFLATION
M × V ≡ P × Y
Quantity identity (always true by definition). M = money supply, V = velocity, P = price level, Y = real GDP. P×Y = nominal GDP. V is defined as whatever makes it hold.
m + v ≈ π + y
Growth-rate form of the quantity identity. m = money growth, v = velocity growth, π = inflation, y = real GDP growth.
Uses the approximation: if X = A×B, then x ≈ a + b
π ≈ m − y
Quantity Theory of Money (long-run, assuming v = 0 i.e. constant velocity). Inflation ≈ money growth − real GDP growth.
Works well cross-country long-run; NOT for short-run prediction
LECTURE 1 · GROWTH RULE
Compound: (1+g)ᵗ
Small growth differences compound enormously. U.S. at 1.7%/yr vs. Argentina at 1.1%/yr since 1900 → U.S. GDP/capita $62k vs. Argentina $17k today.
Rule of 70: years to double ≈ 70 / growth rate %
